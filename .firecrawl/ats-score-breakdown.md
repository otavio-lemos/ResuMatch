## What Is an ATS Score and Why Does It Matter?

An **ATS score** (sometimes called a resume compatibility score, resume match score, or ATS compatibility rating) is a numerical percentage that represents how well your resume aligns with what an applicant tracking system needs to correctly read, parse, and rank your application. Think of it as a compatibility grade between your resume document and a specific job posting.

Applicant tracking systems are software platforms that companies use to manage, sort, and filter the flood of applications they receive for every open position. When you submit your resume online, it almost never goes directly to a recruiter's desk. Instead, it passes through an ATS first. The system scans your document, extracts information, matches it against the job requirements, and assigns a score. Only resumes that meet or exceed a certain threshold get forwarded to a human reviewer.

Your ATS score is calculated based on several factors, but the core question the system is answering is simple: **Does this resume contain the right information, in a format I can read, with the keywords that match this job?**

Here is why this matters more than most job seekers realize:

- **75% of resumes are rejected by ATS** before any human being ever sees them. Three out of four applications never reach a recruiter's inbox. If you have been applying to jobs and hearing nothing back, this is likely why.

- **99% of Fortune 500 companies** use some form of ATS to screen applications. This is not a niche technology — it is the standard. Companies like Google, Amazon, JPMorgan Chase, and Pfizer all rely on automated screening.

- The average corporate job posting receives **250+ applications**. Recruiters physically cannot review every single one, so they depend on ATS to narrow the field to a manageable number — typically the top 25-50 candidates.

- **88% of recruiters** surveyed in 2025 said ATS is their primary initial screening tool. Even at smaller companies, ATS adoption has grown rapidly in recent years.

- Resumes that score **85% or higher** are approximately **3x more likely** to result in an interview callback compared to resumes scoring below 50%.

The bottom line: if your ATS score is low, you are being eliminated before a recruiter even learns your name. You could be the single most qualified candidate in the applicant pool and never get a chance because the software could not properly read your resume or found insufficient keyword overlap with the job description.

## What Factors Determine Your ATS Score?

Your ATS score is not a single measurement — it is a composite of several factors, each weighted differently. Understanding these factors allows you to improve strategically rather than making random changes and hoping for the best.

### Keyword Match (30-40% of Your Score)

Keyword matching is the single largest factor in your ATS score. This component measures how well the terminology in your resume aligns with the terminology in the job description. ATS systems are fundamentally keyword-driven — they scan for specific words and phrases that indicate you have the required skills, experience, and qualifications.

Keywords fall into several categories:

- **Hard skills and technical tools** — Programming languages (Python, Java, SQL), software platforms (Salesforce, SAP, HubSpot), frameworks (React, Django, TensorFlow), and technical competencies (data analysis, financial modeling, UX design)

- **Soft skills and competencies** — Leadership, project management, communication, problem-solving, strategic planning, team collaboration, stakeholder management

- **Certifications and credentials** — PMP, AWS Certified Solutions Architect, CPA, PHR, Google Analytics Certified, Six Sigma Green Belt

- **Industry-specific terminology** — Regulatory compliance (for finance), HIPAA (for healthcare), Agile/Scrum (for tech), SEO/SEM (for marketing)

- **Job title variations** — If the posting says "Software Engineer" and your resume says "Developer," some ATS systems may not register it as a match

**How to optimize keyword match:**

Read the job description line by line. Identify every skill, tool, certification, and qualification mentioned. Then confirm each one appears in your resume, using the same phrasing the job description uses. If the posting says "project management," use "project management" — not "managed projects" or "PM." ATS systems are often literal in their matching.

Place your most important keywords in multiple locations: your professional summary, your skills section, and within your experience bullet points. This signals relevance through repetition without resorting to keyword stuffing.

For a comprehensive keyword strategy, read our guide on [how to pass ATS screening in 2026](https://cvcraft.roynex.com/blog/how-to-pass-ats-screening-2026).

### Formatting Compatibility (25-35% of Your Score)

Formatting compatibility measures whether the ATS can physically read and extract information from your resume file. This is where many qualified candidates lose significant points without ever knowing it. Your resume might contain every keyword in the job description, but if the ATS cannot parse the document, none of that content matters.

**Formatting elements that commonly cause parsing failures:**

- **Tables and columns** — Many ATS systems read documents linearly, left to right, top to bottom. Tables and multi-column layouts cause the parser to scramble information, mixing text from different sections together.

- **Text boxes** — Floating text boxes in Word or design tools are often invisible to ATS parsers. Content inside them simply disappears.

- **Images, graphics, and icons** — ATS cannot read text embedded in images. Skill bars, star ratings, pie charts, headshots, and decorative icons are all invisible to the system.

- **Headers and footers** — Most ATS platforms skip the header and footer sections of a document entirely. If your name, email, phone number, or LinkedIn URL is in a header, the ATS may not capture it.

- **Non-standard or decorative fonts** — While most standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman, Garamond) parse without issues, decorative or unusual fonts can cause character encoding problems.

- **Infographic-style layouts** — Resume templates from design platforms (Canva, Figma, Adobe) that prioritize visual appeal over text structure are among the most problematic formats for ATS.

**Formatting that ATS handles well:**

- Single-column layout with clear visual hierarchy

- Standard section headings (Experience, Education, Skills, Summary)

- Simple bullet points (round dots or hyphens)

- Standard date formats (MM/YYYY or Month YYYY)

- 10-12pt font in a standard typeface

- Adequate white space without relying on tables for spacing

For the complete formatting rulebook, read our [ATS resume format guide for 2026](https://cvcraft.roynex.com/blog/ats-resume-format-2026). And if you are wondering whether your specific layout is safe, check our analysis of [whether ATS can read tables and columns](https://cvcraft.roynex.com/blog/can-ats-read-tables-columns-formatting-2026).

### Section Completeness (20-30% of Your Score)

ATS systems are programmed to look for specific resume sections. When a section is missing, the system either docks points or, in some cases, fails to parse the document entirely. Section completeness checks whether your resume includes the standard components that both ATS and recruiters expect.

**Required sections (missing any of these will lower your score):**

- **Contact information** — Full name, email address, phone number, and city/state location at minimum. LinkedIn URL is increasingly expected. Make sure this information is in the main body of the document, not in a header or footer.

- **Professional summary or objective** — A 2-4 sentence overview at the top of your resume that summarizes your experience level, key skills, and career focus. This section is where you should front-load your most important keywords.

- **Work experience** — Job titles, company names, employment dates (month and year), and bullet-pointed accomplishments for each role. This is typically the longest section and should be ordered reverse-chronologically.

- **Education** — Degree name, institution, and graduation date (or expected graduation date). Include GPA only if it is above 3.5 and you graduated within the last 2-3 years.

- **Skills** — A dedicated section listing your technical skills, tools, and key competencies. This section serves as a keyword anchor — it ensures the ATS captures your skills even if they are not repeated in every experience bullet.

**Optional but valuable sections:**

- Certifications and licenses

- Professional development and training

- Volunteer experience (especially for early-career candidates)

- Publications or speaking engagements (for academic or research roles)

- Projects (for technical or creative roles)

### File Format and Encoding (5-10% of Your Score)

The file format you submit your resume in affects whether the ATS can open and parse it at all. While this factor carries the smallest weight in the overall score, getting it wrong can result in a complete parsing failure — effectively a score of zero.

**File format compatibility ranking:**

- **.docx (Microsoft Word)** — The gold standard for ATS compatibility. Virtually every ATS on the market can parse .docx files reliably. If you are ever unsure, default to .docx.

- **.pdf (Portable Document Format)** — Works with most modern ATS platforms (Greenhouse, Lever, Workday, Taleo), but some older or less common systems still struggle with PDF parsing. The critical distinction is between "text-based PDFs" (created by exporting from Word or Google Docs) and "image-based PDFs" (created by scanning a printed document or exporting from design tools). Text-based PDFs parse well; image-based PDFs usually fail completely.

- **.doc (legacy Word format)** — Generally parsable but may lose some formatting. Use .docx instead when possible.

- **.txt (plain text)** — Fully parsable but loses all formatting, which makes your resume difficult to read if it does reach a human reviewer. Use only as a last resort.

- **.rtf, .odt, .pages** — Inconsistent support across ATS platforms. Avoid these formats.

- **.jpg, .png, .gif** — ATS cannot extract text from image files. Never submit your resume as an image.

## How to Read Your ATS Scan Results

After running your resume through an ATS scanner, you will receive a report with multiple metrics. Knowing how to interpret these results — and which issues to fix first — is the difference between efficient optimization and wasting time on low-impact changes.

**Overall Score: Your Headline Number**

This is the single percentage that represents your total ATS compatibility. It is calculated by weighting the individual factors (keyword match, formatting, completeness, file format) according to their importance. Use this number as your benchmark — if it is 80% or above, you are in good shape. If it is below 80%, look at the individual components to identify where you are losing points.

**Keyword Match Rate: Where Most Points Are Won or Lost**

The keyword match section shows you a side-by-side comparison of terms found in the job description versus terms found in your resume. Pay close attention to:

- **Matched keywords** — These are already in your resume. No action needed, but make sure they appear in context rather than as isolated list items.

- **Missing keywords** — These are the terms present in the job description but absent from your resume. Prioritize adding the ones that appear most frequently in the job posting and the ones labeled as "required" rather than "preferred."

- **Partial matches** — Some scanners identify close but not exact matches (e.g., "manage" vs. "management"). These are worth adjusting to exact matches where possible.

**Formatting Flags: The Silent Score Killers**

Formatting issues do not just lower your score — they can prevent the ATS from reading entire sections of your resume. If the scanner flags formatting problems, these should be your first priority. Common flags include:

- "Content detected in header/footer" — Move this information to the main document body

- "Table or multi-column layout detected" — Restructure to a single-column format

- "Image or graphic detected" — Remove any images, icons, or skill bar graphics

- "Non-standard font detected" — Switch to Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman

**How to prioritize fixes:**

Fix formatting issues first (they affect parsing of everything else)

Add missing high-priority keywords second (biggest score impact)

Add missing sections third (completeness improvements)

Fine-tune keyword placement and density last

## 15 Ways to Improve Your ATS Score Fast

Here are 15 proven techniques to boost your ATS score, ordered by impact. Many of these take less than 10 minutes each but can add 5-15 points to your score.

**1\. Mirror the exact language from the job description.** If the job posting says "stakeholder management," use "stakeholder management" in your resume — not "working with stakeholders" or "managing stakeholder relationships." ATS systems often match on exact phrases, not synonyms or paraphrases. Go through the job description keyword by keyword and make sure each important term appears in your resume using identical phrasing.

**2\. Switch to a single-column layout.** Multi-column layouts, sidebar designs, and table-based formatting confuse ATS parsers. Convert your resume to a clean single-column structure where information flows from top to bottom in a single stream. This alone can recover 10-20 points on your formatting score.

**3\. Add a dedicated skills section.** Create a clearly labeled "Skills" or "Technical Skills" section that lists 10-15 of your most relevant skills. This section acts as a keyword anchor, ensuring that the ATS captures your core competencies even if they are not mentioned in every bullet point. Place it near the top of your resume, just after your professional summary.

**4\. Remove graphics, images, icons, and charts.** Skill-level bars, star ratings, pie charts, headshots, company logos, and decorative icons are all invisible to ATS. Remove them entirely. If you want to indicate skill proficiency, use text (e.g., "Advanced: Python, SQL; Intermediate: R, Tableau").

**5\. Move contact information out of headers and footers.** Many ATS platforms skip document headers and footers entirely. If your name, email, phone number, or LinkedIn URL is in a header, move it to the first few lines of the main document body.

**6\. Use standard section headings.** Use headings that ATS systems are programmed to recognize: "Experience" or "Work Experience" (not "Career Journey"), "Education" (not "Academic Background"), "Skills" (not "Core Competencies"), "Summary" or "Professional Summary" (not "About Me"). Non-standard headings can cause the ATS to miscategorize or skip entire sections.

**7\. Include both spelled-out and abbreviated versions of terms.** Write "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" rather than just "SEO." Include "Project Management Professional (PMP)" rather than just "PMP." This covers both the long-form and abbreviated keyword matches.

**8\. Quantify your achievements.** While this does not directly affect ATS scoring in most systems, it improves your chances of impressing the human reviewer who sees your resume after it passes ATS. Instead of "Managed a team," write "Managed a team of 12 engineers across 3 time zones, delivering projects 15% under budget." Numbers catch eyes and demonstrate impact.

**9\. Use standard date formats.** Format your employment dates consistently using either "MM/YYYY" or "Month YYYY" (e.g., "03/2023 - 01/2026" or "March 2023 - January 2026"). Avoid non-standard formats like "Q1 2023" or "Spring 2023" which ATS may not parse correctly.

**10\. Match your job titles to the posting where accurate.** If your actual job title was "Client Success Associate" but the target job description says "Account Manager" and the roles are genuinely equivalent, consider adjusting your title or adding the equivalent in parentheses: "Client Success Associate (Account Manager)." Be truthful — do not fabricate titles — but aligning terminology where accurate improves your keyword match.

**11\. Include relevant certifications with full names.** Do not assume the ATS knows that "PMP" means "Project Management Professional." List certifications with their full name, the issuing body, and the date earned: "Project Management Professional (PMP), Project Management Institute, 2024."

**12\. Use simple bullet points.** Use standard round bullets or hyphens for your lists. Avoid special characters, symbols, or custom bullet styles that might not encode properly across different systems.

**13\. Optimize your professional summary with top keywords.** Your professional summary (the 2-4 sentences at the top of your resume) is prime keyword real estate. Include your target job title, 3-5 of the most important skills from the job description, your years of experience, and one or two standout achievements. This section ensures the ATS encounters your most critical keywords within the first few lines.

**14\. Avoid keyword stuffing.** Modern ATS platforms are increasingly sophisticated and can detect unnatural keyword usage. Do not copy and paste the entire job description into your resume, use white-text keywords, or repeat the same term twenty times. Focus on natural, contextual keyword placement — use each important keyword 2-3 times across different sections.

**15\. Save as .docx for maximum compatibility.** If the job application does not specify a file format, default to .docx. It has the highest parsing success rate across all ATS platforms. If you must use PDF, export from Microsoft Word or Google Docs (not from a design tool) to ensure text is machine-readable.

## Common ATS Score Killers (And How to Fix Each One)

Even well-qualified candidates regularly sabotage their own ATS scores with avoidable mistakes. Here are the most common score killers and how to fix each one.

### 1\. Keyword Mismatch

**Problem:** You are using different terminology than what appears in the job description. For example, you write "customer service" but the job posting says "client relations." You write "managed budgets" but the posting says "financial planning." The ATS does not recognize these as equivalent.

**Fix:** Treat the job description as a keyword dictionary. Identify every unique skill, tool, qualification, and responsibility term. Then revise your resume to use those exact terms wherever you can do so honestly. This is not about lying — it is about speaking the same language as the job posting.

### 2\. Overly Creative Formatting

**Problem:** Your resume looks stunning as a visual design, but ATS cannot read it. Graphics, tables, multi-column layouts, text boxes, infographic elements, and decorative icons all interfere with automated parsing. The ATS may extract gibberish, skip entire sections, or fail to parse the file entirely.

**Fix:** Strip your resume down to a clean, single-column, text-based layout. Use formatting for readability (bold for section headers, bullet points for lists) but avoid anything that requires visual rendering to convey information.

### 3\. Missing or Non-Standard Section Headers

**Problem:** You have used creative section titles like "My Journey," "What I Bring," or "Toolbox" instead of standard headings. ATS systems are programmed to look for specific section names. Non-standard headers cause the parser to misclassify your content or skip sections entirely.

**Fix:** Use universally recognized section headings: "Professional Summary," "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," "Certifications." Save the creativity for your cover letter.

### 4\. Wrong File Format

**Problem:** You have submitted your resume as a .jpg image, a .png screenshot, a .pages file, or a PDF exported from a design tool where text is rendered as graphics. The ATS cannot extract text from these formats.

**Fix:** Save your resume as .docx from Microsoft Word or Google Docs. If you need to use PDF, ensure it is a text-based PDF by verifying you can select and copy text from the document.

### 5\. Critical Information in Headers or Footers

**Problem:** Your name, email address, phone number, or LinkedIn URL is placed in the document header or footer. Approximately 60-70% of ATS platforms skip header and footer content entirely, meaning the system may process your resume without capturing your contact information.

**Fix:** Place all contact information in the first few lines of the main document body. It can be at the very top of the page, but it must be in the body — not in a Word header/footer element.

### 6\. No Dedicated Skills Section

**Problem:** Your skills are mentioned throughout your experience bullets but there is no consolidated skills section. The ATS may not pick up on skills that appear only once in the middle of a long bullet point.

**Fix:** Add a clearly labeled "Skills" section listing your 10-15 most relevant skills. This section ensures every important keyword is captured regardless of how thoroughly the ATS parses your experience section.

### 7\. Inconsistent Date Formatting

**Problem:** Your resume mixes date formats — "2023-2024" in one entry, "Jan 2022 to March 2023" in another, and "Q3 2021" in a third. Inconsistent dates confuse ATS parsers and may result in incorrect tenure calculations.

**Fix:** Pick one date format and use it consistently throughout your resume. "Month YYYY" (e.g., "January 2023 - Present") is the safest and most universally parsed format.

### 8\. Spelling Errors in Key Terms

**Problem:** You have misspelled a critical keyword — "Pyhton" instead of "Python," "Manegment" instead of "Management," "Anaysis" instead of "Analysis." ATS systems match on exact text. A misspelled keyword is a missed keyword.

**Fix:** Proofread your resume carefully, paying special attention to technical terms, tool names, and certification titles. Run a spell-check, but also manually review because spell-checkers do not always catch specialized terminology errors.

### 9\. Overly Long Resume Without Prioritization

**Problem:** Your resume is 4+ pages with every job you have ever held, including irrelevant positions from 15 years ago. ATS systems may truncate extremely long documents, and even when they parse the full document, keyword density (the ratio of relevant keywords to total text) decreases when there is too much irrelevant content.

**Fix:** Keep your resume to 1-2 pages for most roles (3 pages is acceptable for senior executives or academics). Prioritize recent and relevant experience. Remove or condense roles that are more than 10-15 years old or unrelated to your target position.

## ATS Score by Industry: What Different Fields Expect

ATS scoring thresholds and keyword expectations vary significantly by industry. What counts as a strong score in one field might be insufficient in another. Here is what to expect across major sectors.

**Technology and Software Engineering**

The tech industry is one of the heaviest users of ATS, and keyword matching tends to be highly specific. Job descriptions list particular programming languages, frameworks, cloud platforms, and methodologies. A resume for a software engineering role that mentions "JavaScript" but not "TypeScript" (when the posting specifically requires TypeScript) will take a keyword hit. Tech roles also frequently list 15-25 distinct technical skills, so the keyword matching bar is high.

Target score: **85%+** for competitive roles at major tech companies. The volume of applicants at companies like Google, Meta, and Amazon means your resume needs to stand out even at the automated screening stage.

**Healthcare and Medical**

Healthcare positions have extremely specific keyword requirements driven by regulatory terminology, certifications, and clinical specialties. Terms like "HIPAA compliance," "EMR/EHR systems," "BLS/ACLS certification," and specific medical terminology must appear exactly as listed in the job description. Missing a required certification keyword can be an automatic disqualifier.

Target score: **80%+** for clinical roles. Compliance-related keywords are often treated as mandatory rather than weighted, so a single missing certification can have an outsized impact.

**Finance and Banking**

Financial services roles emphasize regulatory knowledge, analytical tools, and specific financial instruments. Keywords like "SEC reporting," "SOX compliance," "Bloomberg Terminal," "financial modeling," and "risk assessment" carry heavy weight. Large banks use sophisticated ATS configurations that also look for specific degree types and certifications (CFA, CPA, Series 7).

Target score: **82%+** for competitive roles at major financial institutions. Regulatory and compliance keywords are particularly important.

**Marketing and Creative**

Marketing roles present a unique challenge because they often value both analytical and creative skills. Job descriptions mix technical keywords (Google Analytics, SEO, A/B testing, marketing automation) with strategic terms (brand strategy, content marketing, demand generation). Creative roles may also request portfolio links, which ATS handles differently from traditional resume content.

Target score: **78%+** for most marketing roles. The keyword variety is broad, so focus on matching the top 10-12 most emphasized terms in the job description.

## Is My ATS Score Good Enough? Real Scenarios Answered

Job seekers frequently ask whether their specific score is high enough to submit an application. Here are direct answers for the most common score ranges.

### Is 75% a Good ATS Score?

A 75% ATS score is moderate — you are above the danger zone but below the comfort zone. At 75%, your resume will likely pass ATS screening for positions with lower applicant volumes (under 50 applications), but it is at significant risk for competitive roles that attract hundreds of candidates. The gap between 75% and 80% is often just 2-4 missing keywords and perhaps one formatting tweak. Spend 30-60 minutes addressing the highest-priority suggestions from your scan report, re-test, and you should be able to push past 80% without a complete resume overhaul.

### Is 79% a Good ATS Score?

You are one point below the recommended threshold. At 79%, you are almost there — this is not a score that requires major surgery. Look at your scan report for the single easiest fix: it might be adding one missing keyword, removing a table that is causing a minor parsing issue, or adding a skills section that was missing. Making one or two small changes should push you to 80%+ with minimal effort. Do not submit at 79% if you can spend 10-15 minutes getting to 80%.

### Is 82% a Good ATS Score?

Yes — 82% is a strong score that should pass the automated screening stage for the vast majority of positions. Your resume is well-formatted, keyword-aligned, and structurally complete. For standard roles, you can submit with confidence. If you are targeting ultra-competitive positions at top-tier companies (FAANG, Big 4, Fortune 100), consider pushing toward 85-90% by refining your keyword usage further. But for most applications, 82% puts you in a solid position.

### What About 60%?

A 60% ATS score means your resume has significant gaps. At this level, there are likely multiple missing keywords, one or more formatting issues that prevent clean parsing, or missing sections that the ATS expects to find. Do not submit applications at 60%. Instead, treat this as a signal to invest 45-60 minutes in a focused optimization session. Start with formatting fixes, then keyword additions, then section completeness. Most job seekers can go from 60% to 80%+ in a single focused session.

## When to Re-Check Your ATS Score

Knowing when to re-scan your resume is just as important as knowing how to scan it. Here are the specific situations where you should always run a fresh ATS check.

**Before every job application.** This is the most important rule. Every job description contains different keywords, requirements, and qualifications. A resume that scores 90% for a "Marketing Manager" role might score 65% for a "Digital Marketing Strategist" role at a different company — even though both are marketing positions. Tailoring and re-scanning for each application is the single highest-ROI habit in a modern job search.

**After making any changes to your resume.** Even small edits can affect your score. Reformatting a section, changing a bullet point, adding a new role, or adjusting your skills list could introduce new issues or resolve existing ones. Always re-scan after editing to confirm your changes had the intended effect.

**When you are not getting interview callbacks.** If you have submitted 10+ applications without a single callback, your ATS score is the first thing to check. A low score means your resume is not reaching human reviewers at all. Scan your resume against the most recent job descriptions you applied to and see if the scores are below 80%.

**When you change career direction or target a new role type.** Pivoting from one job type to another usually requires significant keyword adjustments. The skills terminology for a "Product Manager" role is very different from a "Project Manager" role, even though the titles sound similar. Re-scan your resume against the new target role's job description to see where the keyword gaps are.

**After updating your skills or earning a new certification.** Adding a new certification, tool proficiency, or skill to your resume is a positive change, but you should verify it integrates cleanly with the rest of your document and does not push your resume to an awkward length or displace other important keywords.

**Every 3-6 months for a general health check.** Even if you are not actively job searching, running a periodic scan against a representative job description in your field keeps your resume current and ready to deploy at short notice.
